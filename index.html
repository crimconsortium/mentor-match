A note on this list

Don't make too much of this quiz.

There are many good reasons to choose a mentor, and many good ways to find one. This quiz looks at four things — research topics, location, faculty rank, and open-scholarship posture — because those are easy to compare across hundreds of scholars. It can't see the things that often matter most: how someone advises, whether their lab has funding for you, how their current students are doing, what your conversations together feel like, whether they're taking new students at all, or fit on dimensions we didn't ask about.

Treat these names as a starting point for reading and outreach, not a ranking of who would be best for you. Read their recent work. Email a few. Ask current and former students what it's actually like. Talk to your undergraduate advisors. A name far down this list — or not on it at all — may turn out to be the right person.

How the ranking works

Mentor Match is a transparent weighted matching tool, not a psychometric instrument. We don't claim to predict who will be a good advisor for you — only to surface promising starting points for further reading.

Each faculty member earns points across the four dimensions you weighted. We sum the points, divide by the maximum you could have awarded, and rank everyone. There are no hard vetoes: a scholar can still appear even if they don't perfectly match every dimension, as long as they score well overall. Each card explains exactly why that person rose to the top.

Faculty data is shared with the Criminology PhD Faculty Explorer. Topic tags are editorial — criminology has no universally agreed taxonomy — and match the same canonical list used in the Explorer.
